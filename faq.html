Frequently Asked Questions

“Know Before You Go.”

We want every visitor to have a safe and enjoyable experience at Elder Glen National Park. Below are some of our most frequently asked questions:

GENERAL QUESTIONS

What are the park’s hours?

Elder Glen is open from sunrise to sunset. The park is closed after dark except for registered night tours or registered campers. If you find yourself inside the park after hours, a ranger will assist you. Do not run.

What activities are available at Elder Glen National Park?

Elder Glen National Park offers a variety of activities, including hiking, camping, fishing, and wildlife viewing. There are also several picnic areas and a visitor center with exhibits and information about the park.

Can I explore off-trail?

For your safety and the protection of the environment, we ask that all visitors remain on designated trails. Some paths may appear unmarked or unfamiliar—if you encounter one, kindly turn back.

What should I bring with me to Elder Glen National Park?

When visiting Elder Glen National Park, it's important to bring plenty of water, snacks, and sun protection. You should also bring a map and compass, as well as a first aid kit. If you plan to camp, be sure to bring a tent, sleeping bag, and other camping gear.Supplies can also be bought at the visitor center

What is the entrance fee to Elder Glen National Park?

The entrance fee to Elder Glen National Park is $25 per vehicle. There is no fee for pedestrians or bicyclists. The fee is valid for 7 days from the date of purchase.

What is the camping fee at Elder Glen National Park?

The camping fee at Elder Glen National Park is $20 per night. There is no fee for pedestrians or bicyclists. The fee is valid for 7 days from the date of purchase.

I heard Elder Glen has strange folklore. Is there any truth to it?

Like many historic parks, Elder Glen has its share of local legends. While we encourage visitors to enjoy the stories, we remind everyone that they are just that—stories. There is nothing unusual about the Glen.

CAMPING & OVERNIGHT STAYS

What are the camping options at Elder Glen National Park?

Elder Glen National Park offers a variety of camping options, including tent camping, RV camping, and yurt camping. There are also several group campsites available for larger groups.

What are the camping fees at Elder Glen National Park?

The camping fees at Elder Glen National Park vary depending on the type of camping and the length of stay. The fees are as follows:

  • Tent camping: $20 per night
  • RV camping: $30 per night
  • Yurt camping: $40 per night
  • Group campsites: $50 per night

Can I camp anywhere in the park?

Camping is allowed only in designated areas. If you wake up and find your tent is no longer where you set it up, remain calm and wait for sunrise.

I heard strange whispers outside my tent at night. Should I check?

No.

SAFETY & WILDLIFE

What should I do if I encounter wildlife at Elder Glen National Park?

Observe from a distance and do not engage. If an animal appears to be watching you for an extended period, slowly leave the area. If an animal speaks to you, report it to the visitor center immediately.

Are there bears in the park?

Yes, black bears are occasionally spotted. However, if you see a bear standing motionless on the trail for too long, or if it does not blink, please notify a ranger.

What should I do if I get lost in the park?

If you believe you are lost, stay where you are and wait for assistance. If you feel like the landscape around you has changed or is unfamiliar, close your eyes and count to ten before looking again. This usually helps.

MISCELLANEOUS

Why are some signs in the park scratched out?

Unfortunately, weather and time wear down signs in the deeper areas of the park. If you find a sign that has been completely defaced, do not attempt to read what was there.

I saw someone in old-fashioned clothing deep in the woods. Is there a historical reenactment?

No official reenactments take place at Elder Glen. If you see someone wearing outdated attire, do not approach them. Do not let them see you.

Why do I feel like I’m being watched in certain areas?

Nature has a way of making us feel small in the grand scheme of things.

What happens if I break the rules?

Don't

Flora and Fauna

Flora & Fauna of Elder Glen National Park

Nature thrives here. Despite our best efforts.

Elder Glen National Park is home to a diverse and unique ecosystem, boasting plant and animal species unlike any found elsewhere in the world. While much of its wildlife appears normal at first glance, experienced visitors know to look closer. Some things here do not behave as they should. Some things should not exist at all.

Most of this information comes from the brilliant Dr. Miriam Halloway's research

FLORA: The Forest That Breathes

“Some plants take more than they give.”

Elder Glen’s forests are ancient, filled with trees and plants documented nowhere else. While many appear similar to common species, local researchers caution against direct contact.

The Veilwood Trees (Common, Park-Wide)

The most common tree in the Glen. Veilwood trees are towering, dark-barked trees that seem to lean toward visitors as they pass.

Field Note: Some trees have been found with human-like growths resembling eyes. Do not make eye contact.

The Hollow Ivy (Unusual Growth Patterns, Use Caution)

A vine that grows on the trunks of Veilwood trees. It is known for its unusual growth patterns and its adversion to being observed. It is known to grow in places where nothing else survives. If wrapped around a tree, that tree will never rot—even if cut down.

Field Note: If Hollow Ivy begins growing on your clothing, remove it and do not take it home.

The Rootsnare Vines (Unusual Growth Pattern, Avoid Contact)

Long, twisting, snake-like roots and vines that seem to shift slightly when unobserved. Found along the trunks of The Warden Trees and appear to be an extention of the tree itself. If you see one, do not touch it. It has anti-coagulating properties and will cause you to bleed profusely.

Field Note: Rootsnare Vines are actually Carnivorious and it's cells are made up of both human and animal cells.

The Warden Trees (Extremely Rare, Found Deep in the Forest)

Enormous trees with unusual knotted bark resembling faces. Always appear in groups of three. Always. And If cut, they bleed a dark red sap.

Field Note: The Warden Trees were once people that have been fed to the Elders

FAUNA: The Watchful Wildlife

"Something is wrong here"

The park is home to a variety of native species, some of which have adapted in unusual ways. While many behave as expected, others… don’t.

The Elder Deer (Common, Park-Wide)

Large, antlered deer with a distinctive white patch on their foreheads. They are known for their unusual behavior, such as standing still for extended periods and not blinking. If you see one, do not approach it.

Field Note: Some rangers claim to have seen a stag with a human face. The official stance is hallucination due to forest isolation.

The Echo Birds (Mimicry Experts, Be Careful)

These birds are known for their ability to mimic the sounds of other animals and even human voices. If an Echo Bird repeats something you were thinking but did not say aloud, leave the area immediately.

Field Note: Do not repeat words spoken by an Echo Bird.

The Unseen Hounds (Heard, Never Seen)

These hounds are not confirmed but have been reported by many hikers. Reports of heavy panting and soft footfalls behind hikers at night. No paw prints. No physical sightings. No official confirmation.

Field Note: They hunt those who are lost

Final Field Note: If you find something in Elder Glen that should not exist, do not disturb it. And never bring it home.

Additional Fauna Worth Mentioning

“The Elders have their favorites.”

While not officially recognized, these creatures have been spotted in the park.

Star Racers

Threat Level: Low

Star Racers are tall, anatomically inproportional, lizard-like creatures that move when the stars do.

They are impoosibly fast, yet can only move as the stars do from our perspective. They are actively hunting you but are usually too slow to actually be deemed a threat.

Just hope for no shooting stars.

Sun Children

Threat Level: Medium

Sun Children are humanoid creatures that are regurgitated energy from those who have been fed to the Elders.

They are usually found in groups and are almost undetectable on bright, sunny days.

They hunt for the Elders. Avoid them.

Long Neck Lurker

Threat Level: High

Long Neck Lurker is a large, aquatic, long-necked creature that is known for its aggresive behavoir.

It eats anything that moves. It's hunger can never be quelled.

It is also known to be very territorial.
